Section 3

Who is Flock Safety?

Flock Safety is a private, venture-backed company that sells a subscription surveillance service to police, cities, businesses, and HOAs — not a one-time camera purchase. Typical Flock contracts keep ownership of the hardware with Flock; the city pays recurring fees for installed cameras plus cloud search, alerts, and network access. Public debate is not “anti-law enforcement” — it is residents asking how a private vendor’s database is governed, audited, shared, retained, and paid for with tax dollars.

How the product actually works

Cameras capture an image of every passing vehicle plus time and location metadata. Data uploads to a private cloud dashboard. Agencies subscribe for search, alert, and analytics tools. Standard Flock service agreements state that Flock Hardware remains Flock’s exclusive property — title does not pass to the city — and Flock can remove the devices if the subscription ends. The company makes money from storing, searching, and sharing the data — not from selling the city a camera it owns.

Beyond plate lookups, Flock markets FreeForm: natural-language AI search across LPR and video (“blue SUV with a racing stripe,” “man in blue shirt and cowboy hat”). That is a searchable appearance-and-movement database, not a dumb camera on a pole.

Plain-language definition when people say “Flock sells your data”: Flock sells access to a platform built from public-space captures, plus optional analytics. Even if the vendor frames outputs as “not personal data,” it is still a paid movement database paid for by taxpayers.

Why Crossville residents should care

  • Crossville PD runs Flock Falcons per Quote Q-00234 and Atlas of Surveillance (11 cameras, Sept. 2025).
  • Scans of ordinary drivers can enter the same vendor cloud used elsewhere in Tennessee — including departments with documented personal-use misuse (Oak Ridge).
  • Contract language in other cities has included broad vendor rights to use “Aggregated Data” for marketing and product purposes (see e.g. the public Whitestown, IN Flock services agreement §4.4). Crossville should publish its live agreement so residents can read the local terms.
  • Independent reporting has also documented Flock using overseas gig workers to annotate U.S. camera imagery for AI training — see overseas AI annotation.

Overseas AI annotation of U.S. camera footage

In December 2025, 404 Media reported that Flock used Upwork gig workers — including workers in the Philippines — to review and classify vehicle and people imagery from U.S. cameras to train machine-learning models. Exposed training materials showed annotation tasks for plates, vehicle attributes, and related footage; after 404 Media sought comment, the exposed panel disappeared per contemporaneous coverage.

That is a vendor-trust and data-handling issue for any city on the network: Crossville should publish where local imagery may be processed, under what contracts, and whether council approved overseas human review. Secondary summary: DeFlock Birmingham — The Facts · The Verge.

FreeForm: Google-style search over the camera network

Flock FreeForm™ is the company’s AI natural-language search product. Instead of only typing a plate, an officer can type a description and pull visual matches from LPR and video — across the agency’s cameras and, where sharing is enabled, partner networks. Crossville residents should treat “just license plate cameras” as incomplete marketing until the city publishes whether FreeForm / people-search / video-search features are enabled on the local contract — ask under ask before renewal.

  • Vehicles without a plate: plain-language prompts such as “blue SUV with a racing stripe” or “white F-150 with a ladder in the back.”
  • People by appearance: Flock’s own marketing example is “man in blue shirt and cowboy hat.” A company spokesperson has confirmed FreeForm can search by clothing descriptions.
  • Cross-agency reach + alerts: search shared cameras where authorized; get notified when a match reappears — without watching every feed.
  • Flock’s framing: they say FreeForm is not facial recognition — clothing / accessories / vehicle attributes only. Critics note appearance-based search still enables warrantless tracking of people and cars by look across a networked map. Separately, Flock’s own U.S. patent specification describes broader people-classification embodiments — see patent vs marketing.

“Just type what you’re looking for, like ‘man in blue shirt and cowboy hat,’ and get visual matches instantly.”

— Flock Safety FreeForm product page

Questions for renewal: Is FreeForm (or any natural-language / people-description search) enabled? On which camera types? Who can run those searches? Are clothing/appearance queries logged and audited? Has council approved people-search capabilities separately from basic LPR?

What Flock told the Patent Office vs. what it tells cities

Independent researchers at DeFlock SC flagged a tension worth checking against primary documents: Flock’s public FAQ says LPR products do not use facial recognition and do not identify race, gender, or ethnicity — while a Flock Group Inc. patent describes people-classification and face-matching embodiments that go much further. A patent does not prove that every claimed embodiment is live on Crossville’s cameras today. It does show what the company put under oath to the U.S. Patent Office to protect as IP — and what cities should demand in writing before renewal.

U.S. Patent US11416545B1 (“System and method for object based query of video content captured by a dynamic surveillance network”) lists inventors Garrett Langley and Matt Feury, assignee Flock Group Inc., filing date Oct. 4, 2020, and grant / publication date Aug. 16, 2022.

  • Patent specification (detailed description): describes neural networks that classify people into classes including “male, female, race, etc.,” plus clothing types and estimated height/weight. A worked example says investigators may obtain “face recognition data points” from one camera and query other cameras for matching face recognition data points. Object fingerprints may be treated as the same object when a similarity probability exceeds a threshold (cross-camera matching without a name).
  • Formal numbered claims: the granted claims speak more broadly about classifying “object class” and “object aspect” data and searching on those fields — they do not spell out “race” or “facial recognition” as claim limitations. Treat race / face language as what the specification discloses, not as verbatim claim text.
  • Public marketing (contradiction): Flock’s “Are Flock Products Discriminatory?” page says LPR cameras “do not use facial recognition,” “do not collect demographic information,” and “do not identify race, gender, or ethnicity.” Its rights & safeguards / civil-liberties page repeats that ALPR “does not use facial recognition” and “does not collect or infer race, religion, gender, ethnicity, or other personal traits.” FreeForm is marketed as search “without facial recognition” while still offering people-by-clothing examples (FreeForm section).
  • Already shipping (narrower than the patent’s people demographics): FreeForm’s public product page advertises natural-language people descriptions (clothing) and vehicle attributes across video/LPR — so appearance search is not hypothetical. Demographic classifiers described in the patent are a separate question cities should put in writing.
  • Contract upgrade rights in other cities: Public Flock services agreements commonly let Flock, “in its sole discretion,” make “any upgrades to [the] system or platform that it deems necessary or useful,” stating such upgrades “will not materially change any terms or conditions” of the agreement (example: Glendale, WI Flock agreement §2.12). Crossville has not published whether its live contract contains the same clause — ask under ask before renewal / TPRA.

Questions for renewal: Has anyone on council read US11416545B1? Which people-search / demographic / face-related features are contractually enabled vs. disabled? Can Flock push software upgrades without a new council vote? Will the city publish a written ban on race/gender/ethnicity classification and facial recognition if those features exist in the vendor stack?

Flock’s PR playbook: paid wires, police press-release templates, and sponsored ads

When Crossville first heard about Flock in 2022, the pitch leaned hard on Amber Alerts and finding missing people. That feel-good framing is part of a bigger nationwide pattern: Flock pushes company talking points into public view through paid publicity, fill-in-the-blank police press kits that agencies have published in substantially similar form, and labeled ads in police trade media. Here are three documented ways that happens. (We did not find clear evidence that Flock pays TikTok or Instagram influencers for these stories.)

  1. Flock pays to blast “missing / abducted child” press releases onto the internet. The company sends its own announcements through GlobeNewswire — a service companies pay so their press releases get picked up by news aggregators. Those write-ups then show up on sites like Yahoo Finance looking a lot like regular news. Examples: Flock’s 2021 AMBER Alert / missing-children partnership announcement and a 2026 release about six abducted children recovered in Colorado with Flock plate-reader cameras.
  2. EFF and Techdirt report that Flock provided police PIO templates; several agencies published substantially similar language. The Electronic Frontier Foundation (EFF) and Techdirt report that Flock provides departments a Public Information Officer Toolkit — templates for the police spokesperson (the PIO). One documented template literally reads: “The ___ Police Department has solved [CRIME] with the help of their Flock Safety camera system. Flock Safety ALPR cameras help law enforcement investigate crime by providing objective evidence. … ____ Police installed Flock cameras on [DATE] to solve and reduce crime in [CITY].” EFF criticizes this practice as vendor-driven police messaging when that language shows up as official copy.

Open livestreams: no login, found via commercial search engines

In late December 2025, researchers and journalists documented a real security failure that undercuts “secure company cloud” talking points: Flock Condor people-tracking PTZ cameras were left streaming on the open internet with no username, no password, and no encryption. Anyone who found the URL could watch live video, download roughly 30 days of archive, and reach administrative/debug panels.

  • How they were found: Technologist Benn Jordan located admin interfaces via a commercial device search engine (documented as Shodan queries such as “Flock Admin”). Researcher Jon “GainSec” Gaines expanded the set with Shodan and ZoomEye to about 67 live feeds and debug interfaces — see his full write-up. Jordan’s report describes finding the interfaces “using a commercial search engine” — not hacking into a locked system.
  • Scale: 404 Media verified at least ~60 Condor cameras exposed nationwide; GainSec catalogued 67. Feeds covered playgrounds, parking lots, bike paths, street corners, retail lots, and more. The Verge summarized the same findings.
  • Independent proof: 404 Media’s Jason Koebler stood under a Condor at Harris Road & Young Street in Bakersfield, California, waved, and watched himself on the open livestream while colleagues watched remotely. Jordan filmed himself on Peachtree Creek Greenway feeds that auto-tracked people.
  • What was open: live stream + ~30-day archive download + settings/logs/diagnostics — “like Netflix,” in Jordan’s framing, with the ability to delete footage from the open admin UI (he reported not doing so).

“On my phone, I am watching myself in real time as the camera records and livestreams me—without any password or login—to the open internet.”

— Jason Koebler, 404 Media (Bakersfield Condor verification)

Flock called it a “limited misconfiguration” on a “very small number of devices” and said it was remedied; some agency emails blamed cellular/SIM testing configurations (WFLX / company confirmation). That does not erase the record: people-tracking cameras sold for public safety were briefly a public webcam network discoverable with commercial search tools.

Questions for renewal: Which Flock models are deployed or planned (Falcon vs Condor vs others)? Has the city received written notice of any exposure/incident affecting Crossville or Tennessee partners? What vendor security attestations and breach-notification terms are in the live contract?

51 cybersecurity findings (GainSec whitepaper)

In November 2025, independent researcher Jon “GainSec” Gaines published a formal whitepaper — Examining the Security Posture of an Anti-Crime Ecosystem — consolidating device-level research on Flock’s public-safety stack (license-plate readers, audio/gunshot hardware, compute modules, and related Android apps). The current public release documents 51 findings, including 22 assigned CVEs and additional findings pending CVE assignment, covering authentication, cryptography, and system-design issues (GitHub archive · GainSec summary).

This is separate from the Dec. 2025 open Condor livestream exposure. The whitepaper is about weaknesses in the product line itself — the kind of cameras cities lease — not a claim that Crossville’s units were exploited.

  • Hardcoded credentials: CVE-2025-47823 describes a hardcoded system password on Flock LPR firmware through version 2.2 (NVD / MITRE).
  • Unauthenticated admin API: CVE-2025-59403 is a publicly catalogued Flock camera admin-API issue also cited in this site’s liability table.
  • Device-level control (physical access context): GainSec’s series documents root/debug access paths on Falcon/Sparrow LPR units and related hardware when an attacker can reach the device. Flock’s public response stresses that exploitation would require physical access plus intimate knowledge of internal hardware, and says findings were disclosed earlier in 2025 with CVE registration and ongoing remediations ( Flock response).
  • Account / MFA risk (oversight, not a GainSec device CVE): A Nov. 2025 congressional letter asked the FTC to investigate Flock’s cybersecurity practices, including that MFA was not required and that weak MFA methods remained supported ( Wyden–Krishnamoorthi FTC letter, PDF).

Flock states the compiled findings do not impair customers’ public-safety use, that no customer action is required because Flock owns/maintains the devices, and that remediations continue via hardware and software updates. Residents can still ask whether Crossville received written notice, what firmware/security posture applies to local units, and what breach-notification terms sit in the live contract.

Questions for renewal: Which camera/compute models are installed? Has the city received written notice of the GainSec / CVE findings or related customer advisories? What firmware versions and MFA requirements apply to Crossville PD accounts? Who verifies evidence integrity if a device were physically compromised?

CEO Garrett Langley: the trust problem

Crossville’s contract is with a private vendor whose CEO has a public record residents can evaluate before renewal: an August 2025 company statement acknowledging that some public remarks about federal access were inaccurate and that Flock “communicated poorly,” followed by later quoted comments that cast FOIA requests as a “weapon” and called camera-mapping activists a “terroristic organization.” That is not “anti-police.” It is a vendor-trust issue before any renewal.

1. Acknowledged inaccurate public statements about federal access

After earlier public denials of federal contracts, Langley stated in writing that Flock had run limited pilots with U.S. Customs and Border Protection (CBP) and Homeland Security Investigations (HSI), and that some public statements had been inaccurate. Illinois’s Secretary of State said Flock’s handling of plate data violated state law. If the CEO’s public assurances can be “inadvertently… inaccurate” on federal access, Crossville should not take verbal promises on sharing or retention at face value — publish the contract, audit logs, and agency list.

“…some of our public statements inadvertently provided inaccurate information… we have engaged in limited pilots with the U.S. Customs and Border Protection (CBP) and Homeland Security Investigations (HSI)… We clearly communicated poorly.”

— Garrett Langley, Flock Safety statement (Aug. 2025)

National Lookup Tool: Flock told Congress that about 75% of its law-enforcement customers enrolled in the “National Lookup Tool,” which lets other enrolled agencies search opted-in camera data nationwide (agencies generally must share their own data to use the tool). Crossville has not published whether it is enrolled. Ask under ask before renewal.

Axon and Flock were partners — not always rivals

Most people treat Axon and Flock as separate vendors. They compete for contracts now. For years they were tightly linked:

  • April 2020: Axon announced a commercial partnership and a minority, non-controlling investment in Flock Safety; Axon’s sales team would help sell Flock cameras to police (Axon investor release).
  • June 2021: Axon Fleet 3 in-car cameras launched with two-way Flock integration. Mobile ALPR reads from Fleet 3 appeared alongside fixed Flock cameras inside Flock’s Lookup Experience — one search covering both systems.
  • ~2023: The partnership dissolved after Axon launched its own competing ALPR line. Flock’s public blog confirmed that Flock processed Fleet 3 footage, applied Vehicle Fingerprint technology, and made it searchable inside Flock ((Flock blog).

Switching company names does not erase that shared-platform history. Crossville should ask in writing what Axon products (if any) the city or county already uses, whether any past or present sharing with Flock Lookup was enabled, what data was retained, and under what policy.

Axon can be abused the same way Flock is

Documented Flock and ALPR problems — personal lookups with no case, misread plates, outdated stolen-car lists — are not unique to one company. Axon’s in-car readers, pole cameras, Evidence storage, and Fusus real-time tools can do the same things — and can pull in body cams, private-camera feeds, and AI report writing too. That is why Crossville should not switch companies, and why Crossville should take these cameras down completely and stay away from the same kind of technology under any other name.

Same problems with Flock or Axon

  • More cameras, more places: Readers in patrol cars plus pole/streetlight cameras mean more of your trips get logged

  • Cancel Flock and remove every camera. Do not evaluate, demo, trial, or install Axon — or Motorola Vigilant, Rekor, Verkada, or any other plate-reader system — ever again.

  • Claims like “the city owns the data” or “shorter retention” do not fix this. Taking the cameras down does.

State-level ALPR action: a comparison

What other states have done — and what Tennessee hasn’t. The Fourth Amendment is the floor, not the ceiling. States are free to enact their own additional restrictions on ALPR surveillance. Maine and Virginia have done so. Tennessee has not. The table below summarizes the contrast.

Comparison adapted from the East Tennessee Regional ALPR Transparency Center (Maryville Residents for Privacy).

Protection Maine Virginia Tennessee
Broad prohibition on ALPR use YES (with narrow government / LE exceptions) No No
Data retention cap (ordinary captures) 21 days 21 days (unless tied to active investigation) 90-day max under TCA § 55-10-302 (investigation exceptions); Flock default often 30 days — not a Maine/Virginia-style package
Written agency policies required Yes Yes No
Audit trail and internal audits required Yes Yes No
Mandatory public reporting Yes Yes No
Vendor practice restrictions Yes Yes No
Restrictions on out-of-state data sharing Yes Yes No
Penalty for violation Class E crime Statutory civil and administrative remedies None

What this means for Tennessee residents: the stronger Maine and Virginia protections — shorter ordinary retention (21 days), mandatory policies/audits/public reporting, vendor restrictions, and real penalties — are things the Tennessee General Assembly could still enact. A legislator who refuses those upgrades is choosing to leave Tennesseans with a weaker regime than Maine or Virginia. That is the legislation Cumberland County’s representatives will vote on, oppose, or sponsor.

Tennessee’s retention row above reflects TCA § 55-10-302 (generally no more than 90 days unless retained for an ongoing investigation). That is a storage ceiling, not Maine/Virginia’s protective package: it does not create a broad use prohibition, mandatory written policies, audit trails, public reporting, vendor restrictions, out-of-state sharing limits, or a penalty scheme like the other rows. See also the PEEPS Act effort under Tennessee politician positions.

Section 20

How the Fourth Amendment works — and how ALPR networks bypass it

Traditional path

  1. Specific suspicion of a specific person
  2. Articulate probable cause
  3. Get a warrant from a judge
  4. Then search

Flock ALPR path

  1. You drive anywhere in Crossville
  2. Plate, image, time, and location are stored
  3. In typical Flock deployments, authorized users can type a “reason” and search (Crossville rules unpublished)
  4. You get no notice and no chance to object

TCA § 55-10-302 may limit retention windows, but it does not create a municipal immunity shield against 42 U.S.C. § 1983 claims. Crossville still chooses whether to keep buying the product.

Key precedents & what dense Flock networks look like in court

  • United States v. Jones (2012) — Justice Sotomayor’s concurrence warned that GPS-style monitoring can assemble “a precise, comprehensive record of a person’s public movements” reflecting familial, political, professional, religious, and sexual associations, and that unrestrained government power to assemble that data is “susceptible to abuse.”
  • Carpenter v. United States (2018) — historical cell-site location information generally requires a warrant; the Court recognized privacy in “the whole of [a person’s] physical movements.”
  • Commonwealth v. McCarthy (Mass. 2020) — the Massachusetts Supreme Judicial Court held that the limited bridge-camera facts in that case were not a constitutional search, but warned that with enough ALPRs in enough locations, historic location data “would invade a reasonable expectation of privacy and would constitute a search.”
  • Schmidt v. Norfolk (Flock network; ongoing appellate fight) — court filings and contemporaneous reporting say Norfolk’s ~176 cameras logged one plaintiff 526 times and another 849 times over roughly four months (~4× and ~6× daily), with one unnamed driver logged 14 times in about 6.5 hours, and ~200,000 database accesses citywide (NBC News). A federal judge let the case proceed in Feb. 2025 citing Carpenter-style concerns; later summary-judgment proceedings and appeal are summarized on the ACLU case page.
  • ACLU (July 2025) — Flock’s Investigations Manager / Multi-State Insights / convoy and multi-location search tools are marketed to “detect patterns of suspicious activity” and alert agencies when vehicles appear across states or locations — moving from investigating a known plate toward algorithmically generating suspicion from movement patterns. Crossville should disclose whether those AI pattern tools are enabled locally.

FAQ — Crossville Flock cameras

Do Crossville Flock cameras require a warrant?

No. License-plate cameras scan vehicles automatically. Searching stored travel history is usually a lookup in the police system with a typed reason — not a warrant — unless the city writes stricter rules.

How many cameras does Crossville have?

The 2022 Flock quote covered 10 Falcon cameras. Atlas of Surveillance reports 11 Crossville PD Flock ALPRs as of September 2025.

As of July 21, 2026, this site has not found a city-published official public camera map. The DeFlock map shows community-reported ALPR locations around Crossville via deflock.org. Coverage may be incomplete until more residents report cameras.

How do I request Crossville’s Flock records?

Use the copy/paste TPRA templates and email info@crossvilletn.gov (ATTN: City Clerk), CC’ing the City Manager. Ask for contracts, policies, audits, sharing lists, and camera inventories.

Is it legal to cover my plate to beat ALPRs in Tennessee?

No. T.C.A. § 55-4-110 prohibits tinted/camera-blocking plate covers. See the lawful civic action section for legal alternatives (council, TPRA, DeFlock routing).

Has Flock ALPR been misused in Tennessee?

Yes for confirmed Flock misuse: Oak Ridge officer Zachary Gauthier pleaded guilty and was sentenced after 18 personal Flock searches (among 23 official-misconduct counts) — two years per count with 48 hours to serve, per local reporting. Separately, Institute for Justice reporting says Shelby County Deputy Thadius Gordon was accused of using county ALPR databases (not confirmed here as Flock) more than 100 times to monitor an ex-wife. See Tennessee cases at a glance and the full misuse section.

Is canceling Flock enough if the city hires Axon instead?

No. Multiple cities have dropped Flock and then installed Axon plate readers to keep scanning cars — Denver, Douglas County (CO), Grand Chute (WI), and others. That changes the company name. It does not end mass plate scanning. Axon products raise separate concerns too (AI report-draft tools that delete drafts; an Atlanta ethics case involving an official who owned a stake in Fusus while pitching it). Do not demo, trial, or “evaluate” Axon. Ask council to cancel Flock, remove the cameras, and refuse any plate-reader replacement. Fix this by taking the cameras down and requiring warrants for specific lookups — not by scanning every driver. Full writeup: Not just Flock · Gadget Review (July 20, 2026).

Does Flock track people — or only license plates?

Flock’s core product is vehicle LPR, but FreeForm adds natural-language search that can include people descriptions (for example clothing). The company markets that cameras are not face identification. Crossville should disclose whether FreeForm is enabled before any renewal.

Were Flock livestreams really left open on the internet?

Yes — reporting in late 2025 documented dozens of Condor-class feeds discoverable via commercial search engines with no login. See the open livestream exposure section and its sources.

What are the “51 cybersecurity findings” about Flock?

Independent researcher Jon “GainSec” Gaines published a Nov. 2025 whitepaper documenting 51 findings across Flock’s public-safety device stack (22 with assigned CVEs so far), including hardcoded LPR credentials (CVE-2025-47823). Flock published a response saying many issues require physical access and that remediations continue. Full writeup: 51 cybersecurity findings.

Can Crossville trust Flock’s “immutable” audit logs?

Not as a substitute for city policy. In December 2025 Flock removed officer names, plates searched, and open-text reasons from Network Audit Logs after civilian oversight tools used those fields. Ask Crossville for city-held audit summaries — see audit-log redactions.

Has Tennessee paid a huge ALPR wrongful-arrest settlement yet?

No Tennessee ALPR settlement matching Aurora’s Gilliam case ($1.9M) or San Francisco’s Denise Green case ($495k) — both non-Flock ALPR cases cited for liability scale — appears in the sources cited here. Tennessee’s record so far is felony personal use of Flock (Oak Ridge) and a reported Flock misread stop in Morristown (Herron; Business Insider names a Flock alert). Settlements elsewhere show what cities can face without written rules — see Tennessee’s record so far and liability.

What should I ask before any Flock renewal?

Ask for this first: cancel the contract and remove the cameras. If they will not cancel yet: ask council to announce a yearly hearing ahead of time and take a recorded yes-or-no vote (no automatic renewal that skips a public vote); publish monthly detailed Flock-use reports the public can check (who searched, what they searched, why, hits, and sharing); and publish a written search policy, public map, contract/sharing list, and whether FreeForm is enabled. How you can help: come to City Council and keep asking until they put it on the agenda — see What can I do, other questions worth asking, and meeting times and address.

Does Maine or Virginia have laws Tennessee could copy?

Yes. Maine broadly prohibits ALPR use except for narrow government and law-enforcement exceptions, caps ordinary data retention at 21 days, and makes violations a Class E crime. Virginia requires 21-day deletion unless tied to an active investigation, mandates written agency policies, audit trails, internal audits, public reporting, vendor restrictions, and limits on data sharing. Tennessee has a 90-day storage ceiling under TCA § 55-10-302 but lacks that broader package. See the state-level ALPR comparison.

Does state retention law protect Crossville from lawsuits?

No. TCA § 55-10-302 addresses storage limits; it does not immunize the city from federal § 1983 liability if an ALPR-driven stop violates constitutional rights. That is a central finding in the City Council liability brief on this site.
