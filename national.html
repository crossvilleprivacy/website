Nationwide pattern

Misuse and wrongful-stop cases outside Tennessee

These cases are not Crossville’s. They show how the same kind of plate-reader stack fails elsewhere — false alerts, stalking, and high-risk stops. Crossville still needs a published search policy, partner list, and monthly use reports before any renewal.

Reading this section: Where someone was charged, pleaded, or sentenced, we say so and link sources. Where reporting describes an accusation or investigation without a final public disposition, we label it as an allegation or as attributed reporting — not as a finding of guilt by this site.

Plymouth, Minnesota · June 2026 · Flock false alert / high-risk stop

Joel Feder — tracked for days, then boxed in over a plate that was never stolen

Automotive journalist Joel Feder (The Drive) and his wife were leaving a Kohl’s parking lot in Plymouth when four squad cars boxed them in. Officers ordered hands up; Feder was patted down. Body-cam and Feder’s own account show police had been following Flock plate alerts on the Range Rover press loaner for days, then staged the stop when a camera pinged the Kohl’s lot.

The alert chain started with a bad database entry, not a stolen car. A New Jersey manufacturer plate reported lost in California was logged incomplete as 34 DTM (missing the small middle digits). Feder’s actual plate was 34 10 DTM. According to Feder’s account and later police reporting, the Flock alert did not distinguish those small middle characters, and the network treated the loaner as a hit and kept notifying Plymouth PD. The VIN came back clean; the police report later confirmed the original plate had been lost, not stolen, and was entered wrong.

Sources: The Drive — Feder first-person account · FOX 9 Minneapolis · CBS Minnesota / WCCO · Catalogued as ID 42 in the AI wrongful-enforcement database and FLK-2026-015 in the misuse master database.

Municipal cancellations

Cities that cancelled, removed, or let Flock expire

Cities and agencies across the country have terminated contracts, refused renewal, let agreements expire, suspended cameras, or rescinded approvals. The SF Standard / Ban Flock Cameras tally cited in this site’s cancellations dossier summary reports roughly 82 ended contracts across 28 states (Aug. 2021–May 2026) — a larger aggregator figure than this site’s 42-record verified dossier.

Public pressure has ended contracts elsewhere — but quitting Flock is not always quitting ALPR. In many cities, residents speaking at council helped drive cancellations. Some of those same cities then hired Axon for plate readers on the same poles. That swaps the company name. It does not end mass plate scanning. Read Not just Flock on the Crossville page and Gadget Review — Cities Are Ditching Flock Safety Cameras – Then Hiring Axon to Do the Same Job (July 20, 2026).

Spotlight — company trust and resident pressure

Three examples are especially relevant: a Wisconsin police chief who lost trust after a council misrepresentation; another Wisconsin city that cited public distrust of the vendor; and Staunton, Virginia, where resident opposition plus a CEO email the city rejected helped end the contract.

Spotlight · Oshkosh, WI · 2026-04-23 · Rescinded · ~26 cameras

Oshkosh, WI — rescinded

Council approved a one-year renewal amid vocal public opposition, then unanimously rescinded within ~24 hours after Police Chief Dean Smith said, per local reporting, that Flock misrepresented heat-map tracking capability at the council meeting. Cameras powered off and bagged.

Sources: Post-Crescent · WBAY · CAN-2026-001 in the cancellations database.

Spotlight · Verona, WI · 2025-12 · Non-renewal

Verona, WI — non-renewal

Common Council voted not to renew; contract lapsed December. Officials and residents framed the decision as lack of public trust in Flock Safety. Cameras remained up for months; city covered them pending physical removal.

Sources: Wisconsin Examiner · CAN-2026-002 in the cancellations database.

Spotlight · Staunton, VA · 2025-12-19 · Terminated · ~6 cameras

Staunton, VA — terminated

After months of resident opposition at council (dozen speakers Dec. 11), Chief Jim Williams moved to cancel following a Flock CEO email the city said did not reflect Staunton’s values. Contract terminated Jan. 8, 2026; physical removal lagged.

Sources: Augusta Free Press · WHSV · CAN-2025-003 in the cancellations database.
